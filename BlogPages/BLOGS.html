The readings I selected was 'proposal for a universal electronic system and archive(from literary machines 1981)'. This reading looked at and expanded on the idea presented by Ted Nelson of a ever expanding computer network that contains all the literature of the world. Basically the worlds greatest archive, it explores how the netwokr would be made on both a technical and conceptual level. He called the project Xanadu and wanted it to hold all literature. And to explore that the reading looked into what literature is and the idea of a linkage system. I really enjoyed how the reading defined literature as ongoing system of interconnecting documents. They talked about how all literature is connected even to the point of a child simpling writing something on a piece of paper, because even what they wrote still comes from somewhere. That really shifted my perspective on writing and on a grander scale how I view humanity as a whole and our constant ability to learn and grow as a result of our connections to one another. To connect all these different strains of literature, the reading explores the idea of a linkage system as well as a Eletronic literary systems. Linkage systems were seen as invisible lines that connect different documents together. Think of the search bar feature on Tiktok. When on Tiktok and a video pops, if during that video and throughout the video's comment section a certain group of words or topic is brought up a siginficant amount of times then users have an easy way to search up said topic through two options. One is a grey search bar at the bottom of the video that when pressed by a user will automatically search the words on tiktok. And the other option is if a comment under the video clealry states the words/topic then it will become a blue comment which users can click on to instantly search tiktok for the words or topic. Weather or not users find exactly what theyre looking for is another issue but I think this form of hypermedia is exatcly what Ted wanted for his linkage system of Xanadu. Another topic discussed in the reading I also really enjoyed was the idea of an electronic literatry system. One thing the reading said about this system was that they wanted it to be simple and easy for users to navigate which I found really cool as a design goal because even now in 2025, 20 years since Nelsons theory was released and even more since the release of literary machines 1981 that has always been the goal of those involved in interactive media and user interface. But speaking on what the literary system was. Nelson wanted it that each document not only had multiple copies being easy to access and trace but every change and version to be tracked and saved as well. He believed this would be vital in developing literature as it would allow authors to expand on and look at the different possibilities and shapes all their writing could take. He wanted that changes and different versions of documents had their own index. This also applied to ideas beyond literture, pcitures, videos as well as graphical data structures were to all have the system in place.

Reading of the week: Moulthrop, S. (2003) ‘You Say You Want a Revolution? Hypertext and the Laws of Media (1991)’.

Reflection on the reading:

Moulthrop's "You Say You Want a Revolution? Hypertext and the Laws of Media" is a cultural and socially analysis on the media hyptertext. Moulthrop defines Hypertext as an interactive electronic text, that separates itself from the then dominate text of print through its non linearity. He analyzes hypertext through the lens of Marshall Mcluhan's laws of media to measure hypertext as revolutionary media. He puts hypertext through the 4 questions of, What does the media intensify or enhance, what does it render obsolete/displace, what does it retrieve that was obsolete, and what does it become when taken to its limits? In terms of reflection and comparisons to the internet today I decided to ask all questions about AI. Specifically AIs who are used by the public for information such as Chatgpt and DeepSeek. These AI's have taken center stage when it comes to internet discourse.

    What does it intensify/enhance?

Hypertext: Moulthrop believed hypertext enhanced connectivity, interactivity and reader agency. When compared to print at the time, hyptertext gave readers a lot more options where and when they're getting their information from. Hypertext also encouraged reader participation in terms of the spreading of knowledge. It also help balance the scale in terms of who had control over knowledge at a time when print was ran by major publishing corporations

AI: AI enhances the speed at which we receive more information as well as it packages the information in such a way that it's easier to process. AI also learns and adapts from the user, creating a personalized learning experience. In terms of issues of revolution, AI's sort of customer first position, aids in the development of dangerous thought echo chambers. A huge issue on the internet today.

    What does it render obsolete/displace?

Hypertext: Displaced linear, print based reading. Moulthrop however, did push the arguement that Hypertext also challenged TV even more so than another text media, books, he saw hypertext and books as media that drew their engagement from text and used that deep engagement to challenge the passive nature of Tv. In the end however, due to political and economic factors, most hypertext was reduced to one to many broadcast, search engines like Google or Yahoo for example, leaving hyptertext in the passive state it once challenged.

AI: Ironically speaking AI threated what hypertext became, these massive one to many search engines. AI threatens search engines and other places where people can manual get information, that includes libraries which already took a huge hit from search engines themselves. Which is dangerous because AI don't have proper verification mechanisms like academic journals. AI aso threatens real life jobs of researchers assistants and journalists. While hypertext of the of the past was once a source or even platform for such occupations.

    What does it retrieve that was obsolete

Hypertext: Hyptertext brought back networking and community in terms of the gathering of knowledge. Print culture at the time had taken away the interconnectedness of how knowledge was gathered, large publishing agencies had control of what was said, who said it and when.

AI:AI brought back dynamic conversationally learning. Not in its truest form because of its bias towards the users, as in its not willing to go out of its way to inform the user but more so satisfy them. However, it did bring back the idea of knowledge being built from specific questions being asked and them being answered critically with a combination of researched answers. In terms of comparing AI and Hypertext, hypertext was much better for communal learning a form of learning that, yes, has the potential to be less conversationally but has more potential to be deeper and more memorable for the user.

    What does it become when taken to its limit?

Researching and doing the reading lead me to understand that in a capitalist society, the spreading maintaining and gathering of knowledge and media that excel at that will always fall into the hands of the powerful and wealthy. Both these media when taken to their limits are unfortunately at worst, tools to maintain hegemony and at best under the control of those who's best interest lies in the maintaining of hegemony. Both forms of media should be decentralized and democratized for the people and from their we'll truly see their potential and thw limits they can reach.
