The Age of Misinformation Leaves Marginalized Communities Under Threat

In today’s digital age of social media, information has never been easier to not only access but spread. Several platforms allow billions of user’s accesses to quick and easy personalized information. However, a question does arise of the information’s legitimacy, because as much as these platforms can hold discussion for scientific facts, studies and well documented information, they also leave room for opinions, discourse and most importantly misinformation. This essay aims to look at the negative effects of this misinformation specifically on marginalised communities online. Specifically, the case study for this discussion will be the narrative of the “white genocide” happening in South Africa. This case study will be investigated through lens digital divisions and shadows; terms used in Society and the Internet: How Networks of Information and Communication are Changing Our Lives () by M. Graham and W.H. Dutton. To begin, it would be worthwhile to discuss what misinformation in the digital age looks like.

The Age of Misinformation

Misinformation is the defined as the act of giving false information. The age of misinformation however is much more complex. Max Fisher of The New York times writes “We are in an era of endemic misinformation — and outright disinformation.” (Fisher, 2021) He goes on to explain how there are psychology factors that come into play into the spreading of misinformation. However, throughout this essay they’ll be a focus on the role the digital divide plays in the spreading of both misinformation and disinformation due to factors like the lack of representation online. While not the focus of the essay, disinformation is another term that will be mentioned throughout, and the difference between misinformation and disinformation is the user’s intent, while misinformation focuses on the accuracy of the information, disinformation has the purpose to mislead.

AfriForum, Elon and the Dangers of the Digital divide

Examples of disinformation can be found within the first case study, the spreading of the agenda of a white genocide in South Africa. Though not a relatively new topic, discussions of a possible genocide of white South African farmers has recently sparked a lot of discussion due to its real world impact. In May 2025, President Cyril Ramaphosa attended a meeting at the white house with Donald Trump. The meeting was centred around South African political party, AfriForum, claim of white South African farmers being killed disproportionally to the rest of the population. This resulted in massive political tension between South Africa and the United States causing major socio-economic implications such as the removal of USAID out of the country, a service dedicated to funding programs on HIV/AIDS in South Africa. M.Graham speaks on the idea of the whether the amount of content being created online in different places is changing over time. He looks at the increasing rate of African and Asian users on large platforms like Facebook. He then goes on to explain how that has a temporal shift on the platforms where these users create content. This shift effects a wide variety content on these platforms, for example with the introduction of South African users on to the platform TikTok, Amapiano, a popular genre of music out of South Africa, has taken the app by storm. A wider range of new users brings new perspectives to spaces related, to art, science and specifically psychology.

With the second inauguration of President Donald Trump, radical right spaces began to gain real traction online, this, alongside the rise of anti-intellectualism online became an issue that spread worldwide. Related to this is the fact that there is uneven production of digital information distributed across different geographies (Graham, 2014). Meaning Western and powerful nations such as the US tend to dominate online spaces. This played a huge role in connecting right wing radical thought spaces of the US to the same spaces in South Africa. This coincided with political controverses, such as the recent land expropriation act, comments made between political party the EFF’s leader Julius Malema and Tesla and X owner Elon Musk who at the time had close relations to Donald Trump. These major political incidents invited a lot of discourse surrounding the state of the political relationship between South Africa and the United States. These discourses drew a bridge between the countries radicalized right wing and specifically for actors spreading the narrative of a “white genocide” in South Africa. Despite this connection between these thought spaces not necessarily causing a temporal shift for social media platforms like Twitter and TikTok, the discourse and conversations held as a result, caused real world implications that effected real world people and communities. Specifically, those of marginalised communities. According to UNAID, the removal of USAID resulted in major service disruptions, clinics were closed and vulnerable groups such as adolescent girls, young women and, pregnant women were left at risk to no access to necessary HIV prevention medications like PrEP.

Could this have been prevented?

In Making a Feminist Internet in Africa: Why The Internet needs African Feminists and Feminism, writer Sheena Magenya predicts a world in which after the covid 19 virus pandemic and its subsequent socio-political effects. “Without enough voices of reason and care for difference and diversity, we are in danger of people emerging a month after quarantine more conservative and more radicalised than before, through accessing an internet that represents a dominant and oppressive minority.” (Magenya, 2020) Despite missing the window of the covid 19 lockdown to expose the internet to more voices of reason and diversity, Magenya’s claim still holds true. Without these voices involved in the discourse of what was occurring between the US and South Africa, real world actions were taken, and vulnerable communities were affected. Another factor to consider are the data shadows of South Africa and specifically this topic. Data shadows refer to the layers of digital information about places (Graham, 2014). Despite conversations and discourse happening on different social media platforms like Twitter and Tiktok related to the political tension between the US and South Africa and USAID, alternative spaces online, including journalistic sites and other media-based platforms didn’t have much information to offer on these topics. This lack of real representation across a wide range of online platforms resulted in even more disinformation to be spread as a political tool for radical right-wing supporters.

The age of misinformation reveals fractures in the online digital space, fractures that have the potential to disproportionately harm marginalized communities. When global narratives are shaped by dominant, Western voices, misinformation even has the potential to be a political tool. As shown in the “white genocide” narrative, disinformation can spill into geopolitics with life-threatening consequences. The withdrawal of USAID in South Africa was not just a political outcome, but a humanitarian one, resulting in the harming and risk of young and vulnerable women battling AIDS in South Africa. Without meaningful participation from diverse and local voices online, especially African feminists as Magenya urges, these injustices will persist. Only by confronting digital inequalities through platform reform, digital literacy, and supporting real online local representation, can we begin to build an internet that safeguards rather than threatens the communities most in need of its protection.
