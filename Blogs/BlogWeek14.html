Practicing Ethics

Justice and Ethics in artistic practice

Justice and ethics in artistic and programming practices

Approaching justice and ethics in both artistic and programming practices requires a balance between personal responsibility and collective accountability. At the heart of ethical design is the recognition that technology and creative expression are never neutral—they reflect values, reinforce power structures, and shape the lived experiences of those who interact with them. One of the most important principles in this process is representation and voice. As artists and developers, we must center the experiences and perspectives of marginalized communities while actively questioning dominant narratives. It is not enough to include diverse stories—we must do so ethically, ensuring that consent is sought when real experiences are referenced, and that individuals or communities are not reduced to symbols or aesthetic inspiration. This also means drawing clear lines between cultural appreciation and cultural appropriation. We have a responsibility to understand the context, history, and weight behind the cultural material we draw from, and where possible, to collaborate with or credit origin communities.

Ethical design is also about understanding the difference between intent and impact. Even work created with the best intentions can cause harm if it reinforces stereotypes, erases lived experience, or privileges some voices over others. As such, creators must be open to critique and willing to revise or even withdraw work if it proves harmful. Professional ethics demand that we listen, adapt, and remain humble in the face of feedback—especially when it comes from those most affected. In the field of user experience (UX) and digital design, platforms like Geeks for Geeks emphasize the importance of ethical frameworks that are inclusive, transparent, and aligned with social values (GeeksForGeeks, 2022). These frameworks extend to protecting user privacy and data. A responsible designer must ensure that user information is secure and not exploited for commercial or political purposes. This kind of transparency builds trust between creators and users, affirming the idea that the internet should be a public good, not a tool of surveillance or manipulation. Accessibility is another core pillar. Ethical design must serve everyone, regardless of ability, identity, or background. This includes designing for users with disabilities, ensuring that websites are navigable by screen readers, use semantic HTML correctly, and avoid visual layouts that exclude certain users. Accessibility also extends to language, content clarity, and culturally responsive design. As professionals, we should proactively seek feedback, conduct user surveys, and include marginalized voices in our research to ensure our work is equitable and human-centered

Crucially, ethical practice also involves recognizing the persuasive power of design. As artists and developers, we influence how people behave and make decisions—sometimes subtly, sometimes overtly. With that power comes the potential for manipulation. It is essential that we do not use design patterns that deceive or exploit users, such as dark patterns or forced opt-ins. Instead, our work should empower users to make informed choices and respect their agency. Finally, our ethical obligations extend beyond human-centered design to include environmental responsibility. In the digital age, even virtual projects have real-world consequences. The energy used to power servers, stream media, or mint NFTs contributes to environmental degradation. Artists and developers must consider the carbon footprint of digital work and seek sustainable platforms and tools whenever possible. In sum, ethical artistic and programming practice demands a commitment to inclusivity, transparency, responsibility, and reflection. We must constantly ask ourselves: Who is this for? Who might this harm? Whose voices are missing? Our work should strive not only to be engaging or innovative, but just—and in doing so, contribute to a digital world that is more equitable, sustainable, and human.
